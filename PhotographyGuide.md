# Arrowhead Fly Photography Guide

Capturing high-quality images of small flies (especially midges) can be challenging. Here is a guide to getting professional results with just your smartphone and a few simple items.

## 1. The Setup (Macro Essentials)

*   **Lighting**: Natural, indirect light is best. Avoid direct sunlight (too harsh) or yellow indoor bulbs. A desk lamp with a "daylight" LED bulb (5000K) is a great alternative.
*   **The "Lightbox"**: You don't need a pro setup. A white piece of poster board or even a clean sheet of paper curved against a wall creates a "seamless" background.
*   **Stabilize**: Small flies require extreme focus. Even tiny hand shakes will blur the image. Lean your phone against a solid object or use a cheap smartphone tripod.

## 2. Shooting Techniques

*   **Macro Mode**: Most modern iPhones and Androids have a "Macro" mode that activates automatically when you get close. Look for the little yellow flower icon.
*   **Focus Lock**: Tap and hold on the fly on your screen to "AE/AF Lock." This prevents the camera from hunting for focus while you take the shot.
*   **Negative Space**: Don't just zoom in 10x. Take the photo from about 4-6 inches away at 2x zoom. This often results in a sharper image with a more "premium" depth of field.

## 3. Composing the "Arrowhead Look"

To keep the website looking premium, follow these composition rules:

*   **The Hook Angle**: Photograph the fly from the side, slightly elevated (45 degrees). This shows the profile and the bead/head clearly.
*   **The "Field" Shot**: Occasionally take a photo of the fly in its natural habitat—on a damp rock, a piece of moss, or even a fly box lid. This adds authenticity to the brand.
*   **Consistency**: Use the same background and lighting for all "Catalog" shots so the Hatch Guide looks uniform.

## 4. Post-Processing (The "Pro" Touch)

*   **Contrast & Sharpness**: Slightly bump the contrast and "Definition" in your phone's default editor.
*   **White Balance**: If the white background looks blue or yellow, use the "Warmth" or "Tint" sliders to make it a true, clean white.
*   **Crop**: Crop the image so the fly is the hero, but leave enough "breathing room" around the edges.

> [!TIP]
> If you're struggling with the phone's lens, consider a "Clip-on Macro Lens" for smartphones (like Xenvo or Moment). It's a $30 investment that makes size 22 midges look like giants.
